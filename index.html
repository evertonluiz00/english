Difference between

Economic (adjective): relating to trade, industry and money.

  • The country has been in a very poor economic state.
  • The government's economic policies have led us into the worst recession in years.

Economical (adjective): not using a money, energy, fuel, etc.

  • There's increasing demand for cars that are more economical on fuel.
  • What's the most economical way of heating this building?

Every day (adverb): the end of the sentense.

  • I go to school every day.

Everyday (adjective):

  • On the weekends, I buy everyday products.

Fun (noun): enjoyable, pleasure or entertainment.

  • Have fun (enjoy yourself).
  • We had great fun at the beach.

Funny (adjective): humorous, causing laughter, made me laugh.

  • Do you know any funny jokes?
  • It's a really funny movie.
  • It's not funny, don't laugh!

Make (verb): to produce something, often using a particular substance or material.

  • She makes all her clothes.
  • He made a chocolate cake.

Do (verb): to perform, take part in, or achieve something.

  • What are you doing over the weekend?
  • The only thing we can do now is wait.

Resolution (going to) (noun): a promise to yourself to do or to not do something.

  • I'm going to learning English.
  • I'm going to work for an international company.

Prediction (will) (noun): a statement about what do you think will happen in the future. In prediction sentenses, the subject usually has little control over the future.

  • The pandemic will go to end.
  • I will come back to Canada for vacation.

Rise (natural) (verb): to move upwards.

  • The ballon rose gently (up) into the air.
  • At 6 am we watched the sun rise.

Raise (somebody does the action) (verb): to lift something to a higher position.

  • He raised the window and leaned out.
  • The government plan to raise taxes.

Watch (verb): to look at something for a period of the time, specially something that is changing or moving.

  • Movement - Focused
  • I had dinner and watched TV for a couple of hours.
  • He spent the entire afternoon watching a cricket match.

Look (verb): to direct your eyes in order to see.

  • No movement - Focused
  • Look! There's grandma.
  • They looked at the picture.

See (verb): to be conscious of what is around you by using your eyes.

  • Movement or no movement - No focused
  • Turn the lights on so I can see.
  • I can see you..

Really: use with adjective or verb.

  • It is really hot outside. (adjective)
  • She really likes video games. (verb)

Very: use with adjective.

  • It is very hot outside. (adjective)
  • She very likes video games. (verb)
Gerunds vs Infinitive

Gerund: reading

  • Reading helps you learn Enlgish. - subject
  • Her favorite hobby is reading. - complement
  • I enjoy reading. - object

Infinitive: to learn

  • To learn is important. - subject
  • The most important thing is to learn. - complement
  • He wants to learn. - object

90% of the time, you will use a gerund as subject or complement of a sentense.
